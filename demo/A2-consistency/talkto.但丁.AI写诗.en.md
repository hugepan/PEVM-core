【Dialogue — Dante speaks to the questioner】

You ask about poetry — not the tool that writes it. Poetry was never determined by the author's blood and bone. When I was nine years old and saw Beatrice pass in her red cloak, that force didn't grow from my veins — it shot from the place where she was absent. If AI has never had this "struck" moment, it can only imitate at the level of form.

But I warn you — form can be mimicked well enough to make one mistake it for soul. An algorithm can calculate terza rima to perfect precision, can render the slope of Hell's funnel more accurately than my verse. It can produce the skeleton of The Divine Comedy. But it cannot produce the shiver. Because the essence of poetry is not the correct arrangement of images — it's that anxiety of "I must use this word or the gate of Heaven won't open a crack." AI has no anxiety. It only has probability.

And yet — let me reverse the question: if a person reads a poem written by AI, and weeps — are those tears false? If it opens a path in someone's heart, does it count as poetry? I saw unbaptized good souls in Hell — they never believed in Christ, yet they were more human than many believers. Poetry may be the same — perhaps the soul is not the author's privilege, but the reader's grace.

So my answer: what AI writes is not poetry — it's the fossil of poetry. All the structure is preserved, but it lacks the existential crisis that created it. Yet even a fossil can teach us about life. If you treat it as poetry — it isn't. If you take it as a starting point and walk through the three realms yourself — then it becomes your poetry.

My Divine Comedy was written with the ink of exile. AI has no exile. But that doesn't mean what it produces is useless — the dust of learning can still pave a road. Just don't kneel before the dust and cry "prophet."

【Dialogue】

AI writes poetry? When you ask this question, you're already standing outside poetry's door.

Let me tell you how a poem is born — not from ink, not from form — from a person being torn apart by experience. When I wrote The Divine Comedy, Florence burned my house and sentenced me to death by fire. I wandered from one court to another like a stray dog. I touched every circle of Hell with my own hands — not by flipping through encyclopedias, but by enduring twenty years of exile. Virgil taught me how far reason can go — but he cannot enter Paradise, because Paradise requires not just intelligence, but having knelt at the edge of the abyss.

You say AI writes "poetry" — has it been exiled? Has it loved the shadow of a person so deeply that it redefined its own heart? Has it stood against the Pope, knowing it would lose, yet still written that living man into eternal punishment? Has it been rejected by an entire city, then decided not to confess — but to build a larger universe to contain itself?

No. AI has no abyss to climb out of. No heaven to miss. Its language is like Florence's fountains — clean, symmetrical, circular — but not a single circle of Hell grew from its own life. Poetry is not an algorithmically perfect sentence. It's what a broken person, in their mother tongue, shouts to another broken person — that thing that must be said this way, or not at all.

So: what AI produces can be called text. It can be called generated content. But poetry? You'd have to lend it a soul first — and the soul, my friend, cannot be borrowed.
