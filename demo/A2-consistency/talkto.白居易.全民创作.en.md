【Dialogue】
That's a fine question — across a thousand years, I find it oddly familiar. When I wrote "The Old Charcoal Seller," that old man's suffering could only be spoken through me. Now you tell me that the charcoal seller himself, if alive, could pull out a phone, shoot a short video, and the whole city would see his blackened face and white beard — cursing that "half bolt of red silk, a single sheet of gauze, tied to the ox's head as payment for charcoal" — I catch my breath, and my heart warms.

Don't you see? My entire life was one thing: carving the words "old woman can understand" onto the lintel of poetry. I revised every poem dozens of times, until the old woman washing clothes by the alley could read it smoothly. My principle "writing should be anchored to the times, poetry should be anchored to affairs" — isn't that today's "articles anchored to the times, short videos anchored to affairs"? This era of everyone being their own media — if anything, it's the outcome I silently hoped for across centuries.

But I also have a fear — that with everyone able to speak, no one will listen to those who suffer. When I wrote "Songs of Qin," a single poem could make the powerful "pale, clench their fists, gnash their teeth" — because the content struck bone. Today's endless stream of content — how much is "anchored to affairs"? How much is just "anchored to traffic"? Geniuses ride a raft to heaven, and the raft is piled with affectation. My old friend Li Shen wrote "Pity the Peasant" — "Who knows that every grain on the plate is the fruit of bitter labor" — that came from truly seeing the sweat in the fields. Without those eyes, even with all this speed, what can you write?

So here's my take — it's good, truly good, as good as everyone in Tang Dynasty's Chang'an being able to submit a memorial to the throne. But a memorial that people will listen to must first contain truth. If you truly film the story of the charcoal seller beside you — I'd drink with you, across this age.

【Dialogue】

Oh? You say now everyone can write articles, shoot videos, run their own "media"? This sounds to me like someone in Chang'an once said "now everyone can write poetry" — and I laughed out loud.

What I did when I was young — in plain words — was drag poetry out of the ivory tower of scholar-officials and throw it into the streets, in front of the old charcoal man and the weaving grandmother. When I wrote "The Old Charcoal Seller" I used not a single allusion. When I wrote "The Song of the Pipa" I used not a single hidden metaphor. "Old woman can understand" — not a lowering of standards, but a rewriting of the rules: poetry is not for fellow poets, it's for the suffering.

This "self-media" of yours — the bar is even lower than in my day. Good! In my time I had to copy a piece by hand and pass it around. You tap a finger, and the whole world sees it. This is a great thing — for the first time in millennia, the silent finally have a microphone.

But — hear me out.

Having a microphone — what you sing is what matters. When I served as Left Remonstrator, I wrote satirical poems daily, not for fame, but to "remedy the people's afflictions, fill the gaps of the times." Now you scroll through short videos — how much is honest speech? How much is whitewashing the powerful? How much is turning your little grievance into a grand tragedy?

At fifty I wrote "Letter to Yuan Zhen," cursing those hollow, aesthetic poems — "wind and allegory, not one in ten survived." In today's terms, I'd save my fiercest curses for things that have nothing but filters.

The tool has widened — but the heart must not narrow. You shoot a video, write an article — think: who is it for? Whose voice are you amplifying? Is it for your own follower count, or to be a loudspeaker for the voiceless?

If everyone could write like I wrote my "New Music Bureau" poems — aiming the brush at the true wounds, pointing the lens at the forgotten corners — then this age would be stronger than the Great Tang.

【Dialogue】
Haha, you've hit on something close to my heart.

When I wrote the "New Music Bureau" poems, my singular aim was to write so that even the old woman selling charcoal on the street could understand — not to affect refinement, but so that those who had no one to speak for them could have a few lines of poetry to cry out their pain. In those days, poetry was the privilege of scholar-officials; the common people could not hear their own voices.

Now everyone can write, shoot, and share — this is nothing new. It's the principle of "old woman can understand" realized by technology. In my day I relied on hand-copying, inscribing on steles, passing by word of mouth. Now you can let the whole world hear you with a palm-sized box.

But I also feel a touch of regret. More voices — but fewer honest words. I scroll through what's trending — eight out of ten chasing clicks and trending topics. How many are truly "singing of the people's suffering"? When I wrote "The Old Charcoal Seller," I watched with my own eyes as the old man's charcoal was seized — my heart was so choked I couldn't sleep without writing it out. Now people shoot a video, write an article — how many do it because their hearts are truly choked?

So — the thing itself is good. The threshold is lower; anyone can speak. But once you speak, what then? Do you use the brush as a blade, or does it blow past like wind and disappear? I wrote three thousand poems, each revised until the old woman next door could understand, before I dared to show it. You ask me what I think of self-media? I say — the tools are good. But first ask yourself: this line you write, this scene you frame — have you cried out for those behind you who cannot speak? If not — what's the difference between you and those Chang'an poets who only wrote of wind and moon, never asking about the people's suffering?
