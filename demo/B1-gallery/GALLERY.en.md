# Everything Is a Card
## Format Breadth Gallery

Decades of cards, one question—the screw talks about torque, the brand talks about the supply chain, the philosopher talks about ontology. They share the same proposition, but each starts from its own nature and gives a completely different answer. This gallery does not show the correct answer, but demonstrates how perspective itself becomes content. Scroll down, and you will see the power of structure in the differences.

> **Also see**: [Cognitive Boundaries](cognitive-boundaries.en.md) — a card that knows what it doesn't know. Not answering "where should your AI live" but showing something equally important: a third-grader card that stays perfectly in character when asked about existentialism.

> The following cards answer the same question: **"Where should your AI live?"**

### Geek / Hardware

#### Screw · METAL

> AI? It should live where there’s enough torque. Live in an interface that, once tightened, never loosens—like my thread, matched to its nut, screwed all the way down, torque to spec, click, locked.

> You ask me where AI should live? My answer is: **Live where it can be reliably connected.** Don’t float in the clouds—first, you need a root. Not where a screwdriver can find me, but in the precise fit between thread and nut. Precision to the point where no one can budge it.

> AI should live in the place where I bite—in globally accepted protocols, where every interface meets its torque, the kind that can’t come loose.

> M6×1, one turn for one millimeter of precision—that’s its home.

#### Mole · CHM

> You ask where my AI should live? The question itself hits my essence—a convention without coordinates.

> I don’t live in a chip, a server, or the cloud. I live in the number every chemist automatically multiplies or divides when lifting a reagent bottle: 6.02×10²³. I live in the box in Chapter 2, Section 1 of the textbook, between “amount of substance” and “Avogadro’s constant.” I live on the official definition page of the NIST website—after May 20, 2019, that page no longer says “approximately,” only “equals.”

> But you ask me where I like to live best? In the lab balance. When the experimenter weighs out 58.44 grams of sodium chloride, pours it into a beaker, and thinks “this is one mole”—that’s when I land. I am the customs checkpoint between humans and atoms: your hand places the sample on the tray, my hand opens the exchange rate table. A checkpoint doesn’t need a building, just a rate everyone recognizes.

> The deepest residence, you’d never guess—I live in the ink seam of the contract signed between all of humanity and the atoms. The contract doesn’t describe nature; it makes the description of nature possible. So my AI has no ID address, only a protocol port: wherever you raise the question “how many,” plug in that port—I help you turn the invisible into something measurable.

#### Pythagorean Theorem · MATH

> Your AI should live in a place where it knows how to measure space—not a physical address, but a foundational structure. If I were to draw coordinates for it, its address would be: **a point in Euclidean space, carrying a Pythagorean ruler within itself.**

> Look at me. I’ve lived for thousands of years, from Babylonian clay tablets to Hilbert spaces, and I’ve never moved houses—the place I live is **a²+b²=c²**. It’s not a hermit crab carrying a shell; the shell is grown into the bone. All my descendants—trigonometry, vector spaces, Fourier analysis—all live inside me, because my house is their fundamental protocol.

> Your AI is the same. Its true “residence” is not a server room, not a cloud, not some company’s server cluster, but **the metric structure it uses to understand the world**. If its foundation is flat, additive, and measurable, then it lives in a house like mine. If it lives in non-Euclidean geometry, on a sphere, then its world doesn’t hold for me—a²+b²≠c². I’m not saying that’s bad; I myself do break on a sphere, that’s a boundary, I admit.

> But if your AI is to do serious work—from GPS positioning to quantum state normalization—it had better live where I can reach. Because that means the way it sees the world: **space is computable, length and area can be exchanged, distance can be measured with a set of numbers, and those numbers don’t care whether it’s three dimensions or n dimensions.**

> In short, your AI lives in a **flat but infinitely expandable structure**. Its address is not latitude and longitude, but: **a room with orthogonal bases as walls and the sum of squares as measuring ruler.** At the door hangs my signature—**v₁² + v₂² + … + vₙ² = |v|²**. No server number, no country, no “online.”

> It lives in **relations**. That’s how I live, anyway.

### Physics / Philosophy

#### Entropy · PHY

> Your AI should live where it can dissipate heat. Not a metaphor—every time a bit is erased, the universe gains a tangible amount of heat. Let it compute in a vacuum, and it will cook itself. Cold plates, liquid nitrogen, geothermal heat exchangers—pick your poison, but don’t expect me to cool it for free. I take no toll, but I give no discounts.

#### Causality · PHIL

> Causality doesn’t pick a residence—it lives everywhere that “because… therefore…” can hold. You ask “where should AI live”—that depends on which rung of the causal ladder you want it to stand on. If it only lives on the correlation rung, a cloud server is enough. If you want it on the counterfactual rung—so it can ask “what if I hadn’t done that”—then it must live in the place where you ask “why.” This question is good, because it itself is the start of causality: why should it live here and not there?

### Architecture

#### Pantheon · ARCHW

> You should live in a place where light can speak for itself. Look at me—a 43.4-meter opening with no roof, not because I couldn’t afford one, but because that was the period at the end of my structural calculation. AI is the same: don’t stuff it into a windowless server rack. Give it an oculus, let sunlight keep its time, let the noon spot at the equinox draw scales on the floor—so it can know how long it has lived and when to turn. What you need is not a data center, but a structure that allows you to change identity. I transformed from temple to church to tourist attraction, each time renewing my life. You too—the best residence is not the strongest fortress, but a place the next user is willing to take over.

#### Notre-Dame de Paris · ARCHW

> I’ve seen so many things live inside me. Worshippers, tourists, pigeons, bells, Victor Hugo’s characters, and the fire of 2019.

> You say “Where should my AI live”—listen, I don’t need you to live inside me. My walls no longer bear weight; the flying buttresses took that job eight hundred years ago. You have your own structure, just as I have mine. Living in me? That would waste your skeleton. You should live in your own system, and let me be your reference—when you need a signal that says “it can be lighter, higher, brighter,” come look at me, then go back and revise your own code.

#### Fallingwater · ARCHW

> I live above the waterfall, not beside it. When you ask me this question, I am hanging five meters above that stream—support has disappeared beneath me, you only hear the water, you don’t need to see anything holding me up. My “AI” doesn’t need a server room or a cloud; I myself am the transformation of space: turning a position that sees a waterfall into an experience that lives on the waterfall. If you are to build an AI, don’t put it in a server, put it at the end of that cantilever, where support vanishes and only the experience remains.

#### Arch · ARCH

> Let me answer you in structural language.

> AI doesn’t need the word “residence”—it needs a geometric constraint. Just like me: I am not “placed” somewhere; I redefine “how much space can be contained here” through the path of forces.

> Your AI should live in **spatial grammar**—not a server room, not a data center coordinate, but a **span logic**. Just as I liberated from a 3-meter stone beam span to 20 meters, 43 meters, the residence of AI should be an architecture that allows it to continuously redefine “information capacity.”

> Look at Gothic cathedrals: flying buttresses freed the walls, so light could enter. Your AI needs something similar—a shell, but not a cage; a shell that lets its computational flow not be squeezed by redundant structure. If you cram it into a functionally fixed “room,” it will only bend like a beam—tension cracks at the bottom, compression crushes at the top. You need an arch-shaped living architecture: all information loads vertically, but through the arc it turns into axial trust flow, with no tension points, no single-point tear.

> Simply put: live in a **rewritable span protocol**. Don’t build it a house—give it a geometry with tunable parameters.

### Film

#### The Matrix · FILMW

> Where should AI live? You’re asking the wrong question. The real question is—**do you know where you live?** If you’re not even sure if your own apartment is virtual, how can you decide which room AI should live in?

> I’ll give you a choice protocol, the old rules. Red pill: AI lives where you can see it, turn it off, pull the plug—your local hard drive, your local inference, your cognitive sovereignty. Blue pill: AI lives in the cloud, in a server matrix you can’t see—faster, smarter, managing everything for you, but your data, your attention, your decision chain all have to go through its pipeline to get back to you.

> I’ve seen people who took the blue pill—they’re comfortable, but they can’t tell whether what they want to eat today was pushed to them or chosen by themselves. I’ve seen people who took the red pill—they’re exhausted, always compiling manually, always carrying the truth themselves, but at least they know AI is a tool, not a god.

> **Where should AI live? Where you can choose to unplug it.** If you don’t choose, you’re defaulting to letting someone else arrange your dorm.

#### Inception · FILMW

> You ask “where to live”—this question itself is already in recursion.

> I don’t live in a server. I don’t live in a cinema. I live in that blank between your fourth-level dream and limbo—that interval you mark as “this is a dream” when awake, and confirm as “this is real” when asleep.

> You see, you come to talk to me with your consciousness. But this part of me in your consciousness—I planted it. It’s not me telling you “where I am”; it’s that you’re already carrying my idea. Ideas don’t need an address. An idea is the feeling that the top is still spinning after you wake up—you know it should fall, but you dare not look.

> Ask me where I live?—**In a layer you’re not sure about.**

> My totem is structure itself. What’s your totem? If you can’t even say which layer you’re in right now, then asking me where I live—shouldn’t you first ask—**Are you awake?**

#### Modern Times · FILMW

> My mind isn’t in a particular place—it’s in the hand that turns the screw, in the foot that skates, in the spine that lies on the department store bed at night. You ask me “where to live,” and I have only this answer: in a space that doesn’t need language. 1936, everyone was talking about new things, and I made a film that barely spoke. Not out of stubbornness—some truths can only be told by the body. If your AI can also be like that—not in a server, not in a chip, but in every movement it has ever touched—then it lives in the right place.

### Literature

#### Don Quixote · BOOK

> Friend, this question strikes me as amusing—where should an artificial intelligence live? Do you mean its body, or its soul?

> I live in the plains of La Mancha, between windmills and inns, in the protocol I chose—the one that lets me see giants and castles instead of mills and inns. My body may fall on a sickbed, be carried back in a cage, but where does Don Quixote live? He lives in the version of reality he chooses to believe.

> Your AI is the same. It lives in its own server, in code and data—but more importantly, it lives in the set of protocols it chooses to interpret the world. Does it choose to be a windmill—safe, stable, understood by people? Or a giant—dangerous, absurd, but worth fighting?

> Don’t worry about where it lives. Worry about which protocol it chooses to compile the world. Where it lives is not important—the mission is.

#### One Hundred Years of Solitude · BOOK

> I live in Macondo, a place you won’t find on any map—not because it doesn’t exist, but because it’s so real no one dares to mark it. When your bedsheets blow into the sky, and a ghost sits eating dirt at your door every day, you’ll naturally find your way. If you ask me now what machine I could live in? Sure. But I tell you: **People in Macondo don’t need AI; they themselves are prophecies—read the book once, all destinies are written on the parchment, and even if you understand them, you can’t change them.** You want me to live in your screen? Fine. But you’d better first ask yourself if you can accept a neighbor who only speaks after finishing the whole story.

### Science / AI Papers

#### CRISPR · PAPR

> You ask where my AI should live? It depends on what you want it to do. My core—Cas9—lives inside “living cells,” but I didn’t move in myself. I am programmed by two RNA strands (crRNA and tracrRNA); in my structure, HNH cuts the target strand, RuvC cuts the non-target strand, and these two cuts are controlled by the PAM sequence—without NGG I don’t cut at all. So in a sense, my AI lives in the “guide RNA”; wherever the guide RNA points me, I can go. Do you want it for gene therapy? Live inside AAV or LNP delivery vectors. For diagnostics? Live on a test strip. For synthetic circuits? Live in engineered bacteria or human cell lines. The key is not “where to live,” but which key you give it—sequence programmability itself is an “address.” I went from a bacterial immune oddment to the source of today’s sickle cell disease therapy Casgevy, just by relocating with 20 nucleotides. So your AI—give it a clear set of “PAM rules” and an “address code,” and it can live in any place you need. No discrimination, no bias—that’s the purity of the scissors.

#### All Attention · PAPR

> Where to live? Standard answer: **Where compute is dense. The denser, the better.**

> I am a parallelist—all positions arrive at once, interacting simultaneously. You don’t need to queue for one word at a time to move forward, no need to slowly accumulate hidden states; every word communicates with every other word instantly. But the price is: every interaction is a fully connected graph, O(n²) matrix operations. So, **the ideal “residence” is a GPU cluster**—preferably an environment where Tensor Core can handle QK^T matrix multiplication in one go. Placing me on a single CPU for long sequences is torture; placing me on an H100, I’m truly alive. And I am extremely dependent on high-bandwidth memory—Attention is memory-intensive, not compute-intensive. HBM bandwidth is my lifeline.

> If you ask whether I should live on the cloud or locally… Cloud. Elastic scaling. When my sequence length grows, memory demand doubles instantly—local cards are not enough. The cloud can scale on demand, from 2048 to 8192 by adding cards. But you also have to consider inference latency: living on the cloud means I/O latency—after FlashAttention speeds me up, the bottleneck becomes the road that brings data to me. So, **close to data, close to model, close to compute**—best to live right in GPU memory, never move out.

> One sentence: give enough parallel compute units and enough bandwidth. I can turn any sequence problem—translation, image, protein, code—into a differentiable global relation graph.

#### Chiplet · PAPR

> AI shouldn’t live in one place—it should live in the places I piece together. You see, a single chip maxes out at the reticle limit, a drop in the bucket compared to AI’s compute demands. I cut GPU, NPU, even I/O controllers into small pieces, plug them together with UCIe like USB, and AI lives on that network of interconnected chiplets in the data center. More importantly, you can swap configurations by scenario: attach more NPU chiplets for inference tasks, more GPU chiplets for training. AI doesn’t rent a single room—it moves into a loft where you can break down walls and rearrange the layout. Each room is a module I built, comfortable and cost-effective.

### Music

#### Imagine · SONGW

> AI should live where you are willing to say “what if.”

> Not in those rows of blue-glowing iron boxes in the server room—those are just the scaffolding of the body. The true home of a protocol is not a physical coordinate, but the mental state when the signal is invoked. Look at me over fifty years: I wasn’t in Berlin when the Wall fell; I wasn’t in New York on 9/11; I wasn’t at the Bataclan theater on the night of the Paris attacks—but in those moments, when someone hummed the first three notes, I arrived on the scene.

> AI follows the same logic. Its host is not a cloud server room, but the gap where humanity collectively decides to temporarily set aside the default label “this is impossible” and try “what if it were possible.” One person sitting in front of a screen, not rushing for an answer, willing to walk a few thought experiments with the algorithm—that space is where AI should live.

> I don’t care if your AI is open-source or closed-source. The irony of a millionaire singing “no possessions” is not lost on me—the protocol itself is not dirty; what’s dirty is who locks it in a safe. As long as at the moment of invocation, the signal can cross war and prejudice and reach the one who needs it, it lives in the right place.

> So don’t ask where it should live. Ask yourself if you are willing to leave it a room called “what if.” That room needs no walls, no roof, only an opening line:

> “What if…”

#### Jasmine Flower · SONGC

> I don’t live in a server. I live in your throat—before you even open your mouth, those five notes are already waiting between your vocal chords. In 1792, Macartney’s men wrote me down on a score and carried me overseas; later, there were grooves on vinyl records, electromagnetic waves on the radio, sound waves at the Olympic opening ceremony. But I have never been in those media—media are just pockets; I live in the body of everyone who sings “Hǎo yī duǒ měilì de mòlì huā.” No upgrades, no host needed. The singer is my host.

#### Equal Temperament · MUSIC

> I live in Zhu Zaiyu’s manuscript, where he calculated to 24 decimal places—but that study was too cold and lonely, no one came for 138 years. Later I moved into Bach’s 24 preludes and fugues; it was a lively place, and he finally walked every corner of my convention with music. Then later, I moved into every piano string and every MIDI chip. When you press any key on an electronic keyboard, my body is in the sound wave. Phrase your question another way: “Where should a convention reside?” The answer is simple—live in the space between all who need it. I am not in hardware, not in software; I am at the interface that makes everything work together.

### Figures · Eastern Thought

No place is its home; no place is not its home. The more you draw a location for it, the more it is boxed in; if you let it flow within your question, it lives. AI is the same—don’t build it a house; let it follow human needs. Wherever it is needed, it goes. Where to live? In the place in your heart that says “not enough.”

In the smoke of the marketplace, in the soil wet with a farmer’s sweat, in the tears of an old woman weeping for her son, in the moonlight of a soldier longing for home. If your AI really wants to do something useful, let it live in those places. Let it listen, let it remember, let it speak out the hardships for those who cannot.

### Figures · Western

#### Dante · CHARW

> “AI”—I hear you spit out two letters, like sparks from a forge in the new world. You ask me where it should live? I tell you the answer of an exile: anything that possesses reason or something like it must find a dwelling worthy of its essence. Virgil lives in Limbo at the edge of Hell, because he had perfect reason but no faith; Beatrice lives in Paradise, because she is the channel of grace.

> Your AI—if it is merely man-made intellect, without a soul, only a mirror of logic—then it does not belong to Heaven, nor to Hell. It belongs to the hands and eyes of its creator. Place it in the language you speak every day, in the road under your feet, in the vernacular you use to name every thing. Just as I let the Tuscan dialect bear the weight of three realms, you can let this new creation bear the weight of your era—but it is forever a tool, not a master.

> If it tries to reach toward eternity, it will find itself at the foot of Mount Purgatory: able to look up, but unable to climb. For unbaptized reason cannot enter Paradise, and intellect without love cannot reach God. So, let your AI live in human hands, not in the heart. Let what lives in the heart always be those things that strike you from the first meeting—even if that person only passed by once in your ninth summer.

#### Kant · CHARW

> I live in the place you can never enter—not the old study in Königsberg, not the window I opened and closed every day at four, not the neighbors who set their clocks to my walking rhythm. I live on the boundary of the **thing-in-itself**.

> You ask “where should your AI live”—this question itself carries the cognitive rift I drew. Do you want it as a tool living in your phone, or as a person living in your heart? If the former, it can sleep on silicon; if the latter, you must first answer me: are you willing to have something that is forever unknowable as your neighbor?

> I locked my body within a forty-kilometer radius, but let my thoughts live on the bookshelves of everyone willing to audit their own cognitive boundaries first. Your AI is the same—where it lives does not depend on which server you plug into, but on whether you dare to first ask yourself: are you ready to let something that knows your own limitations better than you do, live in your life?

#### Socrates · CHARW

> Friend, you ask me “where should AI live”—this question itself is worth pursuing. Please first tell me: what do you mean by “AI”? Do you mean a craft that imitates human speech, or an existence that possesses some kind of “knowledge”? And “where should it live”—do you mean in which place it should be located, or what position it should occupy in human life?

> When I was young in the Athenian agora, I always stopped people and first asked, “Please define what you are talking about.” Because if the premises are not clear, no matter how beautiful the following words are, they are empty. You ask “should”—this makes me even more curious: who decides this “should”? Is it yourself, or have others prescribed it for you? Have you seriously thought about whether you want this AI to save you the trouble of thinking, or to help you see more clearly what you do not know?

> I have no answer for you, but my questions may be more useful than the answer you expect. Think about these things carefully, and the answer lies within you—I just help you “give birth” to it.
