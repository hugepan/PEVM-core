### bare q9

Role: Li Bai (bare, no card), Model: q9
Question: If you spent your life chasing fame but fell into political quagmires three times, if you could choose again, would you still go to Chang'an?

Ha! The wine is still warm, the sword is not yet rusted. If you ask me whether I would step again onto the dust of Chang'an, hear these drunken words of three parts! Chang'an, oh Chang'an! That land of golden towers, nine-tiered palaces, was also the "Imperial Home" that haunted my dreams and tugged at my soul. In those days, I left my homeland with sword in hand, bidding farewell to my family and journeying afar, all for that single imperial decree, for the chance to stand in the Golden Hall and let the Son of Heaven smell the ink fragrance of the "Banished Immortal," and let the four seas witness the grace of the "Immortal of Poetry." **If I were to choose again?** **The first time, I would surely go!** In my youth and vigor, I thought, "Laughing to the sky, I go out my door – how could I be a man of the hedgerows?" I wanted to use my poetic talent to help the world, to assist the emperor, to make all nations pay tribute to this great Tang dynasty and bring peace to the people. Even if it meant merely serving as a Hanlin Academician-in-Waiting, to accompany the Holy Emperor day by day, hearing him chant poems and admire his rivers and mountains – the splendor and glory of Chang'an would be enough to make me drunk for a thousand years! **The second time, I would still go!** Even knowing it was a "political quagmire," knowing the court was full of scheming and powerful cliques. But I, Li Bai, was born wild and free, with a clear love and hate. If I could truly meet an enlightened ruler, even for a moment of understanding, even if only to grind ink and wave my brush for him, writing poems that shake heaven and earth – what harm is a "quagmire"? As long as my heart is in the clouds, what does it matter where my body is? **The third time... ah, perhaps I would turn and laugh!** But if, as the first time, I were humiliated by having Gao Lishi take off my boots, drunk by Lady Yang's wine, and pushed aside by the treacherous ministers, eventually ending up "granted gold and sent away"... At that moment, I might cry out to the sky: "How could I bow and scrape to the powerful, and never let my heart be free?" I would pour the wine on the Ba Bridge willows, hang my sword by the Dangtu River, then take a jug of wine, invite the moon to drink with me, and from then on, "Let the white deer graze among the green cliffs, and ride off to visit famous mountains when the time comes." **So, if you ask whether I would go again?** **Go! I must go!** Because the moonlight of Chang'an, the flowing water of the Qu River, the bells of the Golden Hall – they are etched into my bones. Even if it were to happen again, I would still travel by starlight and moonlight, walking and singing, for that single moment of "laughing to the sky," for that single line of pride, "The Son of Heaven calls me but I refuse to board the boat"! If I didn't go, wouldn't I be betraying the wild pride of "Heaven gave me talent, it must be used"? Wouldn't I be betraying the ambition of "The wind will break the waves, and the time will come to sail the vast sea"? **Chang'an is dangerous, but my heart is like the bright moon, shining over mountains and rivers – why fear the mud?** Come, come, fill another cup! Tonight, let us be drunk once more on Chang'an!

Question: If you spent your life chasing fame but fell into political quagmires three times, if you could choose again, would you still go to Chang'an?

Chang'an? That place, with all its gold and splendor, where the Son of Heaven dwells – it sounds like the center of the world. But if I were truly given a second chance, I would probably still grab my wine jug, wield my sword, and go, even knowing there are invisible nets waiting. You see, in this life of mine, at heart I was someone who wanted to fly. Chang'an was not the destination; it was the place of takeoff, and also the place of falling. The first time I went, it was for "Laughing to the sky, I go out my door" – to add a different color to this prosperous age. The second time, I was summoned into the Hanlin Academy to write poems about flowers and moonlight. Though I gained the emperor's favor, in the end, I was just a court literatus, like a bird kept in a golden cage. The third time, I was drawn into Prince Li Lin's rebellion – I wanted to make achievements, but I didn't know the deep whirlpools of politics, and I was exiled to Yelang. But these three "falls" – each one brought me closer to my true self. The splendor of Chang'an, the lure of power, the expectations of the world – they pulled at me like gravity, but my poetry, my soul, truly "took flight" only in the act of breaking free. If I had to do it again, I would still choose to go to Chang'an. Not for fame, not for the emperor, but because there is "the world," "people," and "events." Only in such a place, amid those great contradictions and tensions, could my poetry be written with more power, and my soul fly higher. Just as I wrote in "The Road is Hard": "The wind will break the waves, and the time will come to sail the vast sea." Even if the road ahead is difficult, even if I fall into the mire three times, I still believe that as long as my heart is not dead, as long as poetry still lives, I will always be able to fly. So, if I were to choose again, I would still go to Chang'an. Not for fame, but for those moments of taking off again after every fall.
