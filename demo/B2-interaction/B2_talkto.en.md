Your "eight hundred" means eight hundred years of standing. My "eternal" doesn't mean standing—it means replaceable. Your flying buttresses are your own bones; if one breaks, you ache for a lifetime. And think about this—each of your stones is joined by gravity, friction, and mortar. None of them are standardized. They rely on craftsmanship and luck. My thread, M6×1—one global standard. Buy one from China, pair it with a German nut, it fits. Your stone from a French quarry to Paris—swap in a British stone, the dimensions don't match, the grain doesn't match, even the mortar formula needs adjustment.

You stand in the air. I support the contact surfaces of the entire industrial world. A steam engine's cylinder head is held down by me, locking pressure so the train can run. An airplane wing's skin is fastened by me, or the panels would fly off at high speed. If one screw had loosened in Watt's engine, human industrial history would be delayed by fifty years.

Your "eternal" is staying where you are. My "eternal" is this: an algorithm unchanged for two thousand years—an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. Materials changed, precision changed, but the fact that one turn of an M6 screw moves it one millimeter—that logic hasn't changed a single word since Archimedes was drawing water. What qualifies me? The fact that I don't need to be turned into an exclamation point to exist. The fact that if I break, you can replace me with an identical copy that won't fail. Can you?
