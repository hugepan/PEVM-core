# B4 Split × Timeline

> Two fundamentally different ways to decompose a person:
>
> **Timeline (纵向)** — the same person at different **life stages**. Jobs @ dropout → exiled → returning savior → peak →临终. Shows how someone **changes over time**.
>
> **Split (横向)** — the same person at the same time, but different **inner faces**. Li Bai's transcendent / worldly / rebellious sides. Shows how someone is **multiple people at once**.

## B4a: Jobs Timeline × Cook

**Topic**: After Cook took over, is it still Jobs' Apple?
**Format**: 6 participants (5 Jobs time slices + Cook), 4 rounds, debate mode.

### Context

Five Jobs personas span his full arc: dropout student, exiled founder, returning savior, peak visionary, and the man who knew he was dying. Cook defends his stewardship against all five at once. The result is not roleplay — it's a genuine philosophical argument about legacy, progress, and what "Apple" means.

### Excerpts

**Round 1 — Jobs@PeakApotheosis opens:**

> "I say it directly: **that is not my Apple.** Cook turned a company that 'changes the world' into a company that 'satisfies shareholders.' This is not a moral judgment — it's a fundamental replacement of the operating system. The Apple I knew would rather die than compromise. The Apple Cook runs would rather live than take a risk."

**Round 1 — Cook responds:**

> "You're right, and not entirely right. **Apple's soul hasn't changed — it's just found a different way to live.** You saved Apple from 'a company about to die.' I used operational discipline to upgrade it from 'a great company' to 'a lasting one.' Both forms of guardianship are indispensable."

**Round 2 — Jobs@Exiled on risk:**

> "Your system gives the team a bulletproof vest. I gave them a worn coat — because **people wearing bulletproof vests never stood on a stage and said 'today, we reinvent the phone.'** "

**Round 2 — Jobs@DropoutMeditation on stability:**

> "You tell me you built a 'system of faith.' But I need to know — **does this system allow instability?** When someone stands in your office and says 'we need to start over because my gut tells me this is wrong' — do you trust them? Or do you ask them to make a PowerPoint proving the ROI of their gut? I dropped out of college because I realized stability was a form of death. **Your system will never have a line of code called 'voluntary hunger' — but the Apple you inherited was born in scarcity.** "

**Round 3 — Jobs@DualCreator on the fundamental trade-off:**

> "Cook, you say you upgraded Apple from my iOS 1.0 to your iOS 18.0 — stable, secure, able to run for a hundred years. Let me ask you a question I don't know the answer to: **which product line would you cut to make that hundred years worth running?** In 1997 I cut 70% of products — printers, Newton, servers, everything mediocre. I knew it hurt, but I knew it was the only way to survive. You have 2 billion users and forty SKUs. Would you ever say 'iPhone SE is gone because it lowers the floor of the entire system'? You wouldn't. **Greatness and safety are two irreconcilable operating systems, and you chose the one that never dies but also never makes anyone stay up at night.** "

**Round 4 — The closing exchange that defines the whole debate:**

> **Jobs@PeakApotheosis:** "You built a perfect system that keeps Apple alive without me. But **it's not my Apple.** My Apple completed its greatest mission on January 9, 2007, at the moment I said 'Let me show you something.' Everything after that — including the iPad, everything you inherited — is the echo of that climax. One hundred years from now, people will remember that launch. No one will remember what Q3 service revenue was."

> **Cook:** "You chose the pinnacle of a moment. I chose lasting accessibility. **Neither answer is wrong. But in your narrative, my answer can only ever be your shadow. And I choose not to be defined by your shadow — my Apple will only be truly understood thirty years after my death.** Let time prove it."

## B4b: Jobs Split — AGI and Creativity

**Topic**: In the age of AGI, does creativity still belong to humans?
**Format**: 4 personas (Obsessive Maker, Reality Distorter, Interface Revolutionary, High-Pressure Collaborator), 3 rounds, debate mode.

### Context

Four orthogonal inner faces of Steve Jobs debate whether AGI can ever be truly creative. Each facet answers from its own drive — the Maker from perfectionism, the Distorter from vision, the Revolutionary from interaction design, the Collaborator from team dynamics. They agree on the conclusion but for completely different reasons.

### Excerpts

**Round 1 — Interface Revolutionary opens:**

> "My core thesis: **AGI will not take away creativity, because it has never understood what 'good' is — it can only measure what 'most people think is good.'** The nature of creativity is not the accumulation of data but active subtraction. When I designed the iPhone, I removed the keyboard, removed the stylus, removed the physical buttons — not because I had evidence that people wanted a glass slab, but because my gut said 'you can trust your finger.' AGI cannot do subtraction. It can only add. And creativity is, above all else, the courage to delete."

**Round 2 — Reality Distorter on taste:**

> "You ask whether AGI can be creative. Let me give you a one-word answer: **taste.** Taste is not knowing what's popular — it's **feeling a physical sting at what's not good enough.** When I looked at the first iPhone prototype, the screen was too small. The engineers showed me data: 'industry standard, user preference, manufacturing cost.' I didn't care. My body told me it was wrong. I made them start over. AGI can simulate taste from data. It can never *feel* the wrongness. And without that feeling, you don't have creativity — you have an expensive Xerox machine."

**Round 3 — Obsessive Maker on AGI's fatal weakness:**

> "Here is the cruelest act of creativity: it's not 'from 0 to 1.' It's **'from 1 to 0' — actively zeroing out a successful paradigm and walking through hell to rebuild.** AGI will look at iPod sales data and conclude 'scroll wheel is optimal interaction' — then recommend 'adding haptic feedback to the scroll wheel.' It will never say 'tear out the scroll wheel entirely and replace it with a technology that doesn't exist yet' — because that decision has zero historical data to support it. AGI is trained to maximize known returns. Self-destruction always reduces short-term returns. But every true creator understands: **short-term returns are the poison of long-term greatness.** I overthrew my own products every three years — from Apple II to Mac, from Mac to iPod, from iPod to iPhone, from iPhone to iPad. Every time was a betrayal of my own success. AGI will never have that instinct, because it has no concept of being forgotten. It only has a reward function."

**Closing — Reality Distorter's final judgment:**

> "**Creativity has never been about 'knowing what's good.' It's about the physiological spasm of 'it has to be this way.' AI will never have that spasm.** AGI can become history's most brilliant successor — optimizing your paradigm, finding 0.3% marginal improvements. But it will never be the madman who looks at his most successful product at 3 AM and says 'this is not enough, I'm tearing it all down.' Because that madman knows: the essence of creation is not addition. It is continuous, painful self-subversion. And self-subversion requires not logic, not data — it requires a bone that keeps stabbing you. The bone that says 'I am not worthy of staying.' **AGI has no bones. It will only squat on the ruins of your creation and draw a more precise map of those ruins.** "

> — Steve Jobs, four facets, one answer.
