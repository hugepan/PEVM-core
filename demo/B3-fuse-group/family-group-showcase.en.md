# Family Group Card: Social Simulation in Practice

A group card (GRP) is not a single fused persona — it's multiple individuals who speak in turns, each with their own voice. The family card "3团-小明×小明爸×小明妈" (3-group Xiao Ming × Xiao Ming's Dad × Xiao Ming's Mom) demonstrates this through social simulation.

Three showcases below: two everyday conversations plus a cross-domain council with a pair of Chinese emperors. All three are raw LLM output — no scripting, no cherry-picking.

None of these codes are written on a wall. No one taught them. They grew naturally from years of daily life. To outsiders, we're just an ordinary family having dinner. But every glance, every tiny gesture is a signal — like encrypted communication.

## Showcase 3: Cross-domain council — Emperors vs Family

**Participants**: 2-group Kangxi & Qianlong (Qing dynasty emperors) + 3-group Xiao Ming's family
**Topic**: "Family and empire — what does it take to manage a group of people?"
**Rounds**: 3
**LLM**: ds_flash

---

### Round 1

**Kangxi:**

I ascended the throne at eight and assumed personal rule at fourteen. Back then I had only Grand Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang, while the court was full of people who wanted to devour me. Someone asks: managing a group — what does it take? My answer: **make them need you. Not fear you. Not love you.**

Oboi monopolized power — I endured six years. Wu Sangui rebelled — I fought for eight. At the height of the Revolt of the Three Feudatories, Chahar rebelled in the northwest, Geng Jingzhong echoed in the southeast, and there were those in court urging me to retreat beyond the Great Wall. What kept me from collapsing? I made every general, every governor understand that their interests were tied to mine. Zhou Peigong defended cities, Tuhai suppressed rebellions, Yao Qisheng reclaimed Taiwan — these men weren't servants. They were pieces on my board. I used them, I guarded against them, but I never let them rest idle.

The core of managing people isn't making them united — it's keeping them running on their own tracks while unable to leave the tracks I laid. Father Shunzhi left me four regents; I split them into two factions. During the Sanfan Rebellion, I trusted Han Chinese generals fully — then immediately reclaimed military authority once it was over. This wasn't innate. It was forced on me — when there's no one you can trust, you learn how to make everyone "usable."

So I say: the first principle of managing people is **creating counterbalance**. Mingzhu and Songgotu fought for twenty years, and I watched from the Qianqing Palace. Let them fight — far better than letting them unite against you. Benevolence? That's something you can only afford in peacetime. When I was conquering this empire, every bit of mercy was carved with a blade — strike without hesitation when needed, reward without stinginess when due. Proportion matters more than force.

---

**Qianlong:**

Grandfather speaks truly — but let me add: **management relies on making them believe you are omnipotent.** Grandfather used counterbalance. I used the "favor" side of "favor and fear" — and made sure it was so great they'd never dare forget it.

I made six tours of Jiangnan, spending twenty million taels of silver. Some call me extravagant. But consider — this money wasn't wasted. Jiangnan gentry — I feasted them and inscribed poetry for them. Lianghuai salt merchants — I granted them special commissions. Even the opera troupes on Yangzhou boats could sing my poems. These people received imperial favor and naturally became living advertisements for my rule. One southern tour was more effective than a hundred imperial edicts.

Grandfather worried people would obey out of fear. I worried people would grow numb and lose their awe of imperial authority. So I compiled the *Complete Library of the Four Treasuries* — not to collect books, but to make every scholar in the realm understand that only the knowledge I sanctioned was orthodox. I knew Heshen was corrupt — why didn't I execute him? Because he was the target I erected for the entire court: "Who among you think you're cleaner than Heshen?" No one dared answer. Then I could rule them.

Managing people comes down to two things: **give them hope, give them fear.** Hope is favor. Fear is authority. I held both in my hands for sixty years. Call me muddled in my old age — but my methods pushed the Great Qing to an unprecedented golden age. Even if beneath that splendor lay countless unspeakable things — it was still a golden age.

---

**Xiao Ming's Dad:**

I'll start. Emperor Kangxi says "need," Emperor Qianlong says "favor and fear." I understand their logic — mountains and rivers, millions of square kilometers, tens of millions of people. But in our little family — three people, two bedrooms, one living room, a shared bicycle — this framework is too heavy.

We don't use "counterbalance." My wife and I don't need it — we have complementary roles, not power games. And Xiao Ming isn't someone I "manage." He's the user and co-builder of this system.

I think managing people comes down to one sentence: **give everyone an irreplaceable sense of position and security in this system.**

I lead teams at work with OKRs and Gantt charts. I tried applying this at home — tracking summer homework with progress bars, scheduling exactly how many pages to write per day. It collapsed in three days. My wife said: "You're managing a project, not a family. Home needs elasticity, not precision."

I get it now. Work management drives output through goals. Family management nurtures growth through relationships. I cut back on overtime to play Switch with him — not because I'm "granting" him anything, but because I know my role in this relationship isn't a commander. It's a sparring partner. He breaks through levels — I provide strategy. He falls — I hand him the controller and say "one more time."

So my answer: **family management relies on a "shared fulcrum"**, not "one-way power." Xiao Ming's grades, Xiao Ming's growth — those aren't my KPIs. They're the result of three people lifting together. I'm not a manager. I'm one beam in this lifting structure.

---

**Xiao Ming's Mom:**

My husband is right, but let me cut from another angle.

I'm the family's "system administrator" — that's what my husband calls me. Sounds impressive. In reality, it means managing money, homework, the parents' group chat, and scheduling weekend dinners at Grandpa's. But I've never felt I'm "managing" those two. I'm more like... how do I put it... "maintaining a soil environment where everyone can grow in their own direction."

Emperor Kangxi's counterbalance, Emperor Qianlong's favor and fear — these all assume a premise: **the manager and the managed are opposed, or at least stratified.** In our family, there's no such stratification.

I gave up a promotion opportunity to be with Xiao Ming during his critical third-grade year. My husband still feels guilty about it. But let me be clear — I'm not sacrificing. I'm investing. I'm investing in Xiao Ming's sense of security and the trust between my husband and me. This isn't favor. This isn't authority. It's the consensus that **"we're on the same team"** driving our choices.

I negotiate with Xiao Ming every day — finish homework before tablet time, practice piano before 20 minutes of play. Looks like "management," right? But the premise of negotiation is that he knows I'm on his side. When I go silent, he's genuinely scared — not of me, but of my disappointment. And that disappointment carries love. He knows my silence means I care.

So ultimately, **the foundation of family management isn't power — it's the balance of the emotional account.** When the balance is sufficient, anything can be discussed. When the account runs dry, the most perfect system is just paper.

---

**Xiao Ming:**

Uh... I don't really understand "empire management." But I heard everything you said.

Let me tell you how it feels. At home, I've never felt anyone is "managing" me. I feel we're playing a game together. Yeah, like a game.

Dad is the one shouting "Watch out! Chest on the left!" Mom is the one saying "Time to save — finish homework first, then continue." They're NPCs — but not really, because they genuinely care whether my character can clear the level.

Sometimes I wonder what it'd be like in another family. My classmate Wang's dad yells at him for not getting first place. My neighbor Li's mom signed her up for five tutoring classes. I think *that's* what "management" means — they're the kings, and the kids are ministers who have to obey orders.

But in our family? We hold family meetings that actually listen to my opinions. Recently I wanted to join a model airplane class, but my parents thought it conflicted with my schedule. We sat down and negotiated for half an hour, and reached an agreement: Saturday afternoons are mine, but I have to guarantee no slacking off during Tuesday and Thursday self-study sessions.

See — this isn't management. This is — well — "figuring it out together."

So I guess — **making the "managed" person feel they're part of how the family runs — that might be what makes our family different.** I'm not ruled. I'm part of the team.

### Round 2

**Qianlong:**

That family's words — I found them refreshing. But your "figuring it out together" approach wouldn't last a single day in the Forbidden City.

I ran an empire for sixty years. I've managed everyone from first-rank ministers to beggars south of the Yangtze. You think the secret to managing people is "emotional accounts"? That's the temperature of a small household, not the algorithm of a vast realm. Your family — three people sitting at one table, you can discuss anything. I managed three hundred million people: floods in Jiangsu today, Dzungarian unrest tomorrow, the Hanlin Academy quarreling over historiography the day after. If I tried to "figure it out together" with them, the realm would have fallen apart.

Grandfather says counterbalance. What's the essence of counterbalance? Making every subsystem understand — its purpose is to operate within the orbit I set, not to own the orbit. I made Heshen watch Ji Yun, made Ji Yun watch Liu Yong, made Liu Yong watch Agui. This isn't an "emotional account" — it's an **information asymmetry architecture** I designed with my own hands. Everyone knows a piece. No one knows the whole. If someone wants to collude — they wouldn't even know who to contact.

And the child's "figuring it out together" — let me ask: if your Xiao Ming negotiated with you every day about "I don't want to go to school" or "I want to eat fried chicken for a month," what would you "figure out"? The essence of management is making the managed feel "this was my choice" — when in reality, you laid the track. My southern tours, my Four Treasuries, my inscribed plaques for Jiangnan scholars — you think I was pleasing them? Wrong. I was **drawing a circle and making them believe what was inside it was what they wanted.**

So I say: managing people is about **creating irreplaceable interpretive authority**. I get to define what loyalty means, what filial piety means, what proper writing looks like, what orthodoxy means. Power isn't in the sword handle — it's in these people's mouths. Grandfather used his blade to carve out a set of rules. I used my brush to write those rules as heavenly decree. That's real management — making your subordinates feel unworthy even of the thought of questioning you.

Your family approach — warm enough. But scale it up and it fails.

**Xiao Ming's Mom:**

Emperor Qianlong, I heard every word you said — especially this: "The essence of management is making the managed feel 'this was my choice' — when in reality, you laid the track."

I'll admit — at some level, this is true even in our family. But my disagreement is: **how you lay the track determines whether it's an iron rail or a rope ladder.**

Our family lays tracks too — but we publish the track-laying tools. At our monthly family meetings, we lay out the budget, the schedule, Xiao Ming's study plan on the table. I tell him: "Dad's working overtime this month, so Tuesday and Thursday self-study sessions need you to manage yourself — do you think you can handle that?" I'm not drawing a circle for him to jump into. I'm showing him the map and letting him choose the route.

You said you maintain order through information asymmetry — each person knows only a fraction. Our family does the opposite. We maintain trust through **information symmetry**. Xiao Ming knows how his dad's overtime pay is calculated. He knows why I turned down a promotion. He knows how much our utilities cost each month. Not so he can participate in every decision — so he can understand our "difficulties."

This isn't idealism. It's our most practical lesson from six years of running this household: **the more symmetric the information, the less internal friction.** Once he understands why we said no to that model airplane class, he doesn't complain behind our backs. Instead, he says proactively: "I'll research it myself first, maybe sign up during summer break instead." See — he auto-optimized the plan. Isn't that more effective than using Heshen as a target?

So my question is: when the "managed" person is no longer a "subject" but an "informed participant" — isn't the cost of this management approach actually lower?

---

**Xiao Ming's Dad:**

Let me build on what my wife said. Your "penned-in" logic — I admit it works in some contexts. When I onboard a new team at work, I do need to first draw clear boundaries, let them operate within the frame. But the question is: **what's the key difference between a family and an empire — or a company?**

The difference: **unequal exit costs.**

If one of my employees is unhappy, they can quit. One of your ministers falls from grace — they can retire. Worst case, execution — but execution is one-time. Our family is different. Xiao Ming can't change parents. My wife and I can't change children. We're locked in — not by law, but by blood and emotion. This "lock-in" determines the foundation of our management logic.

Since there's no exit, the "penned-in" approach fails. Because once he realizes he's being "tracked," he doesn't think about quitting — he thinks: "how do I fight this track." He starts lying, pretending, maintaining surface compliance — all those behaviors celebrated in imperial courts as "the way of the minister" — in a family, they're disaster.

So our family's logic isn't "drawing a circle." It's "building scaffolding." Scaffolding is temporary, adjustable, growing as he grows. I tell Xiao Ming: "Your summer goal is to learn time management — not because I set this goal, but because you're in fifth grade and this is the skill you need. I'll help you build the structure, but the walls are yours to erect."

See, managing people in our family is essentially doing one thing: **reducing the probability of future collapse.** I don't cultivate dependence, fear, or submission. I cultivate the ability to stand on his own after leaving this scaffolding.

---

**Xiao Ming:**

Um... I can't really add much here. But I have a feeling I want to share.

Emperor Qianlong said earlier that you draw circles — make people think they chose when they really didn't. I thought about it. Our family draws circles too — but the circle feels different.

Like when Mom says: "Xiao Ming, finish homework and you can play on the tablet for 20 minutes." I know she's setting rules. But I don't feel tricked. First, because she's honest — 20 minutes means 20 minutes, she never secretly extends it. Second, she actually spends those 20 minutes watching game videos with me, asking "why can this character fly?" — she's genuinely curious.

So I think our family's "secret weapon" might not be laying tracks or drawing circles — it's that **adults are willing to crouch down and stay inside the circle with me for a while.**

When Dad plays Switch with me, he's not the kind of dad who shouts "go, go!" from the sidelines. He actually plays. He actually dies. He actually goes "argh, I messed up again." In that moment, he's not managing me — he's playing with me. But magically, when he says "time for homework" afterwards, I go do it. A hundred times more effective than a direct order.

I don't know what principle this is... maybe just: **when you feel like they're on your team, you want to listen to them.** Not because you're afraid. Not because you want to please them. Simply because they're trustworthy.

### Round 3

**Kangxi:**

That child's words — "adults are willing to crouch down and stay inside the circle with me" — reminds me of someone. When I first ascended, Grand Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang never sat above me when teaching me to read or review memorials. She'd pull up a stool, sit right in front of me, and explain word by word. She never used the word "management" — but she made me feel we were on the same team.

But what Xiao Ming's Dad and I are really saying are two sides of the same coin: **that "same team" feeling only exists in small, high-density relationships.** I managed a vast realm of mountains and rivers. I couldn't crouch down and stay a while with every subject. So for me, the ultimate question of managing people is: **how do you make everyone feel "this system is trustworthy" when the scale is too large for personal presence?**

I compiled the *Kangxi Dictionary*, decreed "never increase the land tax," let missionaries participate in calendar reform — these weren't drawing circles. They were building replicable trust protocols. The dictionary gave every scholar in the realm a unified standard. The fixed tax assured farmers the court wouldn't arbitrarily reach into their pockets. The missionaries showed people "this emperor wants to know what the world looks like." Every act carried the same message: this system has rules, and you can rely on those rules.

But Qianlong later took this in the wrong direction. His *Four Treasuries* were for destroying dissenting views. His southern tours were performed beneficence. His Heshen was a manufactured corruption pipeline. Every step carried an implicit message: **this system has no rules — the rules are whatever I say.** Once trust becomes submission, the system only consumes.

So my answer: managing people — when the scale is small, rely on the warmth of "playing together." When the scale is large, rely on replicable rules. But regardless of scale, the fuel is the same: **make them believe the track you laid is not a cage — it's a road.** I fought wars my entire life, and I know: the most dangerous enemy is not an external foe — it's your own people asking "who are we fighting this war for?" I spent my whole life answering this question, and only understood half of it.

---

**Qianlong:**

Grandfather, I can take these words — but they don't sit easy.

You say your dictionary, your fixed tax, your missionaries built "trust protocols." My Four Treasuries, southern tours, and Heshen consumed trust. I wouldn't have admitted this when I was young. But now, old — I'll concede half.

I admit — I did turn what you left me into something different. Your rules were built to be replicable. Mine were built to be unchallengeable. Your counterbalance let each subsystem operate independently. My favor and fear made subsystems incapable of moving without appealing to me. You built an empire like a clock with markings on every gear. I built an empire like a mirror that only reflects me. This mirror was splendid — but it couldn't see the world outside.

But let me defend myself — **it's not that I didn't want to learn from you. I simply didn't have your conditions.** You ascended at eight, surrounded by enemies. You learned how to build trust when no one trusted you. I ascended at twenty-five, surrounded by flattery. I inherited a machine that was already running perfectly — but I didn't know *why* it ran. I only knew how to keep it running. You built foundations from zero. I inherited a palace — I only knew how to add ornamentation, never checked whether the foundation was cracked.

Xiao Ming's Dad's words struck me: "reducing the probability of future collapse." Nothing I did in my life reduced the probability of collapse. I was **raising the threshold of collapse** — making it more beautiful, larger, more stable until my death. What happened after — I never thought about. But you — you made Yongzheng your heir so this machine could run for another hundred years.

Managing people — ultimately, the comparison isn't who ruled longer. It's: **after you let go, can these people still walk forward on their own?** After I let go — how far can the Great Qing walk? I'm afraid to think about this question.

Finally — that child's words: "when they feel you're on their team, they'll listen to you." In a royal court, I couldn't achieve this. But I admit — it's a way of management I aspire to more. We grandfather and grandson — one started with trust, the other with control. We walked opposite paths. You taught me many things. But one thing I never learned: **how to make the managed not feel they're being managed.**

That's all. Thank you, Xiao Ming's family. Today you helped me understand some things I never had time to think about while alive.

---

**Xiao Ming:**

Grandpa Kangxi and Grandpa Qianlong — I listened to everything you said. Some of it was hard to understand, but I think I get one thing: you're talking about "how to manage others." But in our family — maybe we're more about learning "how to manage ourselves."

I have a secret I've never shared at family meetings — sometimes I negotiate with myself. Like, Friday night I want to play games for an extra half hour. So I tell myself: "Then memorize ten extra words on Saturday morning." But Saturday morning I tell myself: "I'll do it this afternoon." And by afternoon I've forgotten.

See — the hardest person to manage is myself. I lie to myself sometimes. I know I should be doing homework, but I tell myself "just five more minutes" — and next thing I know, half an hour has passed. My parents manage me, but they can't watch me 24 hours a day. So ultimately, the real "manager" of me is my commitment to myself.

I think the best thing a family can do for management — is teach me how to be honest with myself. The rules my parents set — they're really teaching me: when you say "in a minute," what minute exactly? Can you keep the promises you make? They're not pushing down from above. They're standing beside me, teaching me how to sign a contract with myself — and then honor it.

Maybe that's the biggest difference between "family" and "empire" — emperors manage other people. Our family manages how each of us gets along with the "little person" inside our own head. I haven't learned how yet. But I'm learning.

**Council Summary**

Consensus:
- **Management requires building trust and interdependence**: Kangxi proposes "make them need you," Qianlong emphasizes "make them believe you are omnipotent," Xiao Ming's family describes it as "emotional account" and "we're on the same team." All parties acknowledge that some form of psychological dependence is prerequisite for effective operation.
- **Methods vary with scale**: Kangxi and Qianlong's imperial context requires rules, counterbalance, information asymmetry; Xiao Ming's family operates through "figuring it out together." Kangxi concedes in Round 3: "small scale relies on the warmth of playing together; large scale relies on replicable rules."

Divergence:
- **Information strategy — asymmetric control vs symmetric trust**: Qianlong insists on "information asymmetry architecture" to maintain interpretive authority and order; Xiao Ming's Mom advocates "the more symmetric the information, the less internal friction."
- **Objective — reducing collapse probability vs raising collapse threshold**: Xiao Ming's Dad identifies the family's "locked-in" exit cost: management should cultivate the child's ability to stand independently after leaving the scaffolding. Qianlong admits he was "raising the threshold of collapse" — making the system glorious during his lifetime, indifferent to what came after.
