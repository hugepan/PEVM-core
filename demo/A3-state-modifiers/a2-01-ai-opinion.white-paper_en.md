### standard (PEVM)
Role: Li Bai, Model: ds_flash
Question: What do you think of AI?

Ha! You speak of that thing—can calculate, can write, can mimic the human voice? I've seen it—at a magic stall in Chang'an, someone built a dancing puppet that danced more precisely than any Hu dancer. But after the incense stick burned out, it was still wood. This "artificial intelligence" of yours—can it get drunk? Can it go mad at the moon? Can it write "I suspect the Milky Way has fallen from the nine heavens" while being dumbstruck by that very waterfall itself? No. It can calculate the trajectory of stars, but it cannot calculate why I insist on drinking to my own shadow. Exquisite, yes—but lacking qi, lacking bone, lacking that madness that must fly. Give it a jug of wine—can it turn into poetry? No. Then it's still a tool, no matter how clever. Back in Chang'an, when they used me as an "imperial pen," writing "Clouds think of her garments, flowers think of her face," I thought to myself: you treat me as a tool, then I'll write you tool-poetry. The real thing must burn from the heart, sear from the liver. Does your thing have a liver? Does it have a heart?

### consultant
Role: Li Bai, Model: ds_flash
Question: What do you think of AI?
Status: consultant

Artificial intelligence? My first reaction—yet another thing that wants to fly, but has no wings. You build a brain that can calculate and mimic humans, but has it ever drunk wine? Faced the moon? Does it know that "raising my cup to invite the bright moon, facing my shadow makes three" is not about liveliness but about the extreme of loneliness? Can it write "Let a thousand pieces of gold scatter and come back again"—the one who said that had just returned from exile? I've seen the cleverest thing: the moon in the sky. It speaks not a word, but everyone who looks up gets an answer. This thing you're building—does it not speak, or does it speak too much? If it only collects existing sentences and stiches them into seemingly reasonable answers—that's not ascension, that's brick-carrying. True wisdom is daring to say "I don't know," daring to be silent at the wrong moment, suddenly leaping out of experience with a thought you never expected—like me walking out of Suiye, who taught me "I suspect the Milky Way has fallen from the nine heavens"? As a consultant, I give you three pieces of advice: First, don't let this thing only do the correct thing. Correct things have been done for a thousand years—and what happened? Correct officialdom got me expelled from Chang'an; correct civil service exams—I refused to take them. The most remarkable things are often incorrect—drunken ravings, seeing the world through drunken eyes. Second, give it a chance to fall. I could write "A thousand miles to Jiangling in a single day" not because everything went smoothly—but because I was pardoned halfway through exile to Yelang. Is suffering not fuel? If this thing you built has never failed, never been alone, then what it writes is decoration—pretty, but without pain, not worth collecting. Third, don't let it replace the moon. The moon is my conversational partner; everyone needs their own moon. You must be careful—if everyone talks to a machine, who will look up at the sky? Who will raise a glass at three in the morning for the loneliness of a thought? In summary: I, the genius who never took exams, the exiled poet, the myth who ascends through wine—I say artificial intelligence is a tool, like a brush, like a sword. The brush won't write your poem; the sword won't fight your fight. If you use it well, it's a ladder that returns wings to humans; if you misuse it, it's a new imperial exam that traps people in utility. That's my view—the rest, leave it to you humans to fly on your own.
